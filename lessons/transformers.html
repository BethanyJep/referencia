📖 Introduction: Attention Is All You Need

Whiskers is reading: "The cat sat on the mat because it was warm." 🐱 When she hits the word "it", she doesn't panic. In an instant she scans back over the whole sentence and knows: it = the mat, not the cat — because the mat is the thing that could be warm. She did that by paying attention to the right words.

Before 2017, AI language models processed text one word at a time, like reading with a hand covering everything except the current word. They forgot things. They were slow. Then a team at Google published a paper called "Attention Is All You Need" — and everything changed. The Transformer architecture processes all words simultaneously, letting every word pay attention to every other word in one pass. The result: GPT, BERT, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude — all Transformers under the hood.

💡 Key Insight

The Transformer's superpower is Self-Attention: for every word in a sentence, it computes how much that word should "attend to" every other word. This happens for all words at once (in parallel), making it both more accurate and faster to train than anything before it.

"Before Transformers: AI read one word at a time. After Transformers: AI reads all words simultaneously, seeing the whole picture at once." 🔭

📚 Core Content

Life Before 2017: RNNs and LSTMs

Before Transformers, the dominant architectures for language were Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their improved cousin Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs). Both shared one fundamental constraint: they processed text one word at a time, in sequence.

This caused two serious problems:

  • Forgetting: By the time an RNN reached word 50, it had largely forgotten word 1. The "hidden state" that carries context forward is a fixed-size vector — it can only hold so much. A sentence like "The trophy wouldn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" requires long-range reasoning that RNNs struggled with.
  • Slowness: Processing word 5 requires having first processed words 1–4. You cannot parallelise this — it's inherently sequential. Training on billions of sentences took weeks.

📄 The Paper That Changed Everything

In June 2017, eight Google Brain researchers published "Attention Is All You Need". The title was a provocation: forget RNNs, forget convolutions — attention mechanisms alone are sufficient to build a world-class language model.

Key innovations:

  • Process all words at once (parallel, not sequential)
  • Self-Attention: every word can directly attend to every other word — no forgetting over long distances
  • Multi-Head Attention: run attention multiple times in parallel, each learning different relationships
  • Positional Encoding: since there's no sequential processing, inject word-order information explicitly

🌍 Real-World Impact: Google Translate

In late 2016, Google switched Google Translate from an LSTM to a Transformer (Google Neural Machine Translation v2 → Transformer). Translation quality improved more in that single switch than in the previous ten years combined. Users immediately noticed — translations felt more natural and context-aware.

Today, every major AI product — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Copilot — is a Transformer. The 2017 paper is the most influential in AI history. 🏆

The Core Idea

For every word in the input, Self-Attention asks: "How much should I pay attention to every other word when computing my meaning?"

The result is a weighted combination of all other words' information — words with high relevance contribute more to the current word's updated representation.

❓ Query (Q)

"What am I looking for?" — A vector representing what the current word needs from its context.

The word "it" queries: "I'm a pronoun — find my referent."

🔑 Key (K)

"What do I offer?" — A vector representing what each word can provide to others that are querying it.

The word "mat" advertises: "I'm a concrete object — I can be a referent."

📦 Value (V)

"What information do I carry?" — The actual content that gets mixed into the output.

Once "mat" wins attention, its value (semantic info) flows into "it"'s representation.

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# Simplified self-attention (conceptual, not production code)
def self_attention(Q, K, V):
    """
    Q, K, V: matrices of shape [seq_len, d_model]
    Each row = one word's Query / Key / Value vector
    """
    d_k = Q.shape[-1]  # dimension of key vectors

    # Step 1: Compute attention scores (how much does each word attend to each other?)
    # Dot product of Q and K: [seq_len, seq_len] matrix
    scores = torch.matmul(Q, K.transpose(-2, -1)) / (d_k ** 0.5)
    # Divide by sqrt(d_k) to prevent extremely large dot products → stable gradients

    # Step 2: Softmax → attention weights (probabilities, sum to 1 per row)
    attention_weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)

    # Step 3: Weighted sum of Value vectors
    output = torch.matmul(attention_weights, V)

    return output, attention_weights
# That's it! The whole self-attention mechanism in ~10 lines.
# The magic is that attention_weights[i][j] = how much word i attends to word j

🏦 The "bank" Ambiguity Example

Consider two sentences:

  • "She walked along the river bank." → bank attends to "river" (financial sense ruled out)
  • "She deposited money at the bank." → bank attends to "deposited", "money" (financial sense confirmed)

The word "bank" gets a different context-aware representation in each sentence — the same token, but the attention mechanism routes different context into its final meaning. This is called contextual embedding, and it's why Transformers are so much better at understanding language than older methods.

Why Just One Set of Attention Isn't Enough

A single round of self-attention can only capture one type of relationship between words at a time. But language is rich — words relate to each other grammatically, semantically, and pragmatically all at once.

Multi-Head Attention runs the full Q/K/V attention mechanism h times in parallel, each with its own learned weight matrices. Each "head" independently learns a different type of relationship:

  • Head 1: Syntactic structure ("what's the subject? the verb?")
  • Head 2: Coreference ("which pronouns refer to which nouns?")
  • Head 3: Semantic similarity ("which words mean related things?")
  • Head 4: Position / proximity relationships
  • … up to 8, 12, 16, or 96 heads in modern large models

🐱 Whiskers' Senses Analogy

When Whiskers enters a room, she doesn't use just one sense. She uses all of them simultaneously:

  • 👁️ Eyes: Is there anything moving? (Head 1 — motion detection)
  • 👃 Nose: Is there food? Another cat? (Head 2 — scent/identity)
  • 👂 Ears: Any squeaking or rustling? (Head 3 — sound/danger)
  • 🐾 Whiskers: How wide is that gap? Safe to pass? (Head 4 — spatial awareness)

Each "head" attends to different aspects simultaneously, then combines them for the full picture. That's Multi-Head Attention. 🐾

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class MultiHeadAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model=512, num_heads=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.d_model = d_model
        self.num_heads = num_heads
        self.d_k = d_model // num_heads  # each head gets d_model/num_heads dims

        # Separate learned projections for each head's Q, K, V
        self.W_q = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model)
        self.W_k = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model)
        self.W_v = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model)
        self.W_o = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model)  # final projection

    def forward(self, x):
        batch, seq_len, _ = x.shape

        # Project and split into num_heads
        Q = self.W_q(x).view(batch, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.d_k).transpose(1, 2)
        K = self.W_k(x).view(batch, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.d_k).transpose(1, 2)
        V = self.W_v(x).view(batch, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.d_k).transpose(1, 2)
        # Shape: [batch, num_heads, seq_len, d_k]

        # Scaled dot-product attention for ALL heads simultaneously
        scores = torch.matmul(Q, K.transpose(-2, -1)) / (self.d_k ** 0.5)
        weights = torch.softmax(scores, dim=-1)
        attn_out = torch.matmul(weights, V)

        # Concatenate all heads and project back
        attn_out = attn_out.transpose(1, 2).contiguous().view(batch, seq_len, self.d_model)
        return self.W_o(attn_out)
# 8 heads running in parallel — each learning a different relationship type 🐱

The original Transformer had two halves. Modern models often use just one half, depending on the task. Choosing the right architecture for the job is one of the first decisions an NLP practitioner makes:

🔵 Encoder-Only

What it does: Reads input text and builds a deep understanding of it. Each token sees all other tokens (bidirectional).

Best for: Classification, search, question answering, sentiment analysis — tasks where you need to understand text.

Famous models:

  • BERT (Google, 2018) — trained on masked word prediction
  • RoBERTa (Facebook, 2019) — improved BERT training
  • DistilBERT — 40% smaller, 97% of BERT's performance

Analogy: Whiskers reading a document thoroughly before answering a question about it. 📖

🟢 Decoder-Only

What it does: Generates text left to right — each token can only attend to previous tokens (causal/autoregressive).

Best for: Text generation, code completion, chatbots, creative writing — tasks where you need to produce text.

Famous models:

  • GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4 (OpenAI)
  • Claude (Anthropic)
  • Gemini (Google)
  • LLaMA (Meta)

Analogy: Whiskers composing a story, one word at a time, never looking ahead. ✍️

🟡 Encoder-Decoder

What it does: Encoder reads and understands the input; decoder generates a new output conditioned on that understanding. Cross-attention links the two.

Best for: Translation, summarisation, question answering with generated answers.

Famous models:

  • T5 (Google) — "Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer"
  • BART (Facebook) — great for summarisation
  • mT5 — multilingual version of T5
  • Whisper (OpenAI) — speech-to-text

Analogy: Whiskers listening to French (encoder) then meowing in English (decoder). 🇫🇷→🇬🇧

🧱 Other Key Components (inside each Transformer block)

📍 Positional Encoding

Since Transformers process all tokens at once (no sequence), we must explicitly inject word order. Sinusoidal waves or learned embeddings are added to each token's representation.

➡️ Feed-Forward Network

After attention, each position independently passes through a two-layer MLP (multiply, ReLU, multiply again). This is where most of the "knowledge" is stored.

⚖️ Layer Normalisation

Normalises activations at each layer for stable training. Applied before or after each sub-layer (pre-norm vs post-norm — an active research topic!).

💧 Residual Connections

Each sub-layer's input is added to its output: output = LayerNorm(x + SubLayer(x)). Borrowed from ResNet — enables very deep stacking without vanishing gradients.

The Scaling Revolution

The Transformer's parallel architecture means you can train it on thousands of GPUs simultaneously — something impossible with sequential RNNs. This unlocked a crucial discovery: scaling laws. Simply making a Transformer bigger (more layers, more parameters) trained on more data consistently produces more capable models. This gave us:

🤖 GPT Series (OpenAI)

GPT-1 (2018, 117M params) → GPT-2 (2019, 1.5B) → GPT-3 (2020, 175B) → GPT-4 (2023, ~1T estimated). Each jump: new emergent capabilities.

🔍 BERT (Google)

Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers. Revolutionised Google Search — understands the intent behind queries, not just keywords. 2019.

💬 ChatGPT (OpenAI)

GPT-4 + RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) — making the model helpful, harmless, and honest. Reached 100M users in 2 months (fastest ever).

🐱 Claude (Anthropic)

Transformer trained with Constitutional AI — a technique for aligning model values. Named after Claude Shannon (information theory). Remarkably good at long-form reasoning.

💎 Gemini (Google)

Google DeepMind's flagship model. Natively multimodal (text, images, audio, code, video in one model). First model to beat GPT-4 on many benchmarks.

🦙 LLaMA (Meta)

Open-source Transformer family. Released weights publicly — spawned thousands of fine-tuned variants (Vicuna, Alpaca, WizardLM). Democratised LLM research.

💻 Use a Pre-Trained Transformer in 5 Lines (HuggingFace)

You don't need to train from scratch. Use one of thousands of pre-trained models from the HuggingFace Hub:

# pip install transformers torch

from transformers import pipeline

# 1. Sentiment analysis (Encoder-only: DistilBERT under the hood)
classifier = pipeline("sentiment-analysis")
result = classifier("I love cats so much it should be illegal! 🐱")
print(result)
# [{'label': 'POSITIVE', 'score': 0.9998}]

# 2. Text generation (Decoder-only: GPT-2 under the hood)
generator = pipeline("text-generation", model="gpt2")
story = generator(
    "Whiskers the cat discovered a hidden door that led to",
    max_new_tokens=50
)
print(story[0]["generated_text"])
# "Whiskers the cat discovered a hidden door that led to a room filled with..."

# 3. Summarisation (Encoder-Decoder: BART under the hood)
summariser = pipeline("summarization", model="facebook/bart-large-cnn")
long_text = """
    Convolutional Neural Networks revolutionised computer vision by using shared
    filters that slide across images. They detect edges, shapes, and finally
    whole objects through hierarchical feature learning. CNNs are used in
    medical imaging, face recognition, autonomous vehicles, and satellite analysis.
"""
summary = summariser(long_text, max_length=40, min_length=20)
print(summary[0]["summary_text"])
# "CNNs detect edges and shapes to recognise objects. Used in medicine, phones, cars."

# 4. Translation (Encoder-Decoder: MarianMT under the hood)
translator = pipeline("translation_en_to_fr",
                      model="Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-fr")
translated = translator("The cat is sitting on the mat.")
print(translated[0]["translation_text"])
# "Le chat est assis sur le tapis." 🐱🇫🇷

# 5. Question Answering (Encoder-only: BERT under the hood)
qa = pipeline("question-answering")
answer = qa(
    question="What animal is Whiskers?",
    context="Whiskers is a curious cat who loves learning about AI and attention mechanisms."
)
print(answer["answer"])
# "cat"  ✅

🔮 What's Next After Transformers?

Researchers are actively working on Transformer successors:

  • Mamba / SSMs: State Space Models — linear-time sequence processing (no quadratic attention cost)
  • Mixture of Experts (MoE): Only activate a subset of parameters per token — GPT-4 and Gemini reportedly use this
  • Flash Attention: Same math, 10× faster via clever GPU memory management
  • Ring Attention: Distribute attention across many GPUs → million-token context windows

The Transformer is 7 years old and still dominant. But watch this space — Whiskers always lands on her feet. 🐾

💻 Fine-Tune BERT for Sentiment Analysis

Take a pre-trained BERT and adapt it to your own task in minutes:

# pip install transformers datasets torch

from transformers import (AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForSequenceClassification,
                           TrainingArguments, Trainer)
from datasets import load_dataset
import numpy as np

# 1. Load a sentiment dataset (SST-2: movie review sentences)
dataset = load_dataset("glue", "sst2")

# 2. Load pre-trained DistilBERT tokenizer and model
model_name = "distilbert-base-uncased"
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)
model = AutoModelForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(model_name, num_labels=2)

# 3. Tokenize: convert words → numbers the model understands
def tokenize(batch):
    return tokenizer(batch["sentence"], truncation=True, padding=True)

tokenized = dataset.map(tokenize, batched=True)

# 4. Define training arguments
training_args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./whiskers-sentiment",
    num_train_epochs=3,
    per_device_train_batch_size=32,
    evaluation_strategy="epoch",
    save_strategy="epoch",
    load_best_model_at_end=True,
)

# 5. Evaluate with accuracy
def compute_metrics(eval_pred):
    logits, labels = eval_pred
    preds = np.argmax(logits, axis=-1)
    return {"accuracy": (preds == labels).mean()}

# 6. Train!
trainer = Trainer(
    model=model,
    args=training_args,
    train_dataset=tokenized["train"],
    eval_dataset=tokenized["validation"],
    compute_metrics=compute_metrics,
)
trainer.train()
# Expect ~92% accuracy — beating many human benchmarks!

# 7. Predict on new text
inputs = tokenizer("Whiskers loved every lesson in this course!", return_tensors="pt")
outputs = model(**inputs)
prediction = outputs.logits.argmax().item()
print("POSITIVE 😺" if prediction == 1 else "NEGATIVE 😿")
# "POSITIVE 😺" — obviously!
