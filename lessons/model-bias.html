📖 Introduction: The Biased Cat Food Detector

Imagine you train a "Cat Food Detector" 🐱🍽️ but all your training photos are of fancy gourmet brands from upscale pet stores. When you deploy it, it works great for expensive brands—but fails completely for regular supermarket cat food!

The model isn't "wrong"—it learned exactly what you showed it. But the training data was biased, and now the model perpetuates that bias. This happens with AI systems in the real world, and the consequences can be serious—from facial recognition that fails on certain skin tones to hiring algorithms that discriminate.

💡 Key Insight

AI models learn from data—if the data is biased, the model will be biased. This isn't a bug; it's how ML works. The responsibility is on us to ensure fair data and fair outcomes!

🐱 The Cat Food Detector Story

Problem: A startup builds an app to identify cat food brands from photos. The developers, being cat enthusiasts with good incomes, only photograph their own expensive brands.

Result: App works great in wealthy neighborhoods, fails completely in regular stores. The model isn't racist or classist—it just learned from biased data!

The fix: Collect diverse training data representing ALL cat food brands, price points, and packaging styles.

Lesson: Your training data must represent the full diversity of your use case!

📚 Core Content

📜 Historical Bias

Bias that exists in the real world and gets captured in data

Example: Training on historical hiring data encodes past discrimination

👥 Representation Bias

Some groups underrepresented in training data

Example: Facial recognition trained mostly on lighter skin tones

📏 Measurement Bias

How we measure or label things introduces bias

Example: Using arrest rates as proxy for crime rates

🔄 Feedback Loops

Model's decisions influence future training data

Example: Predictive policing → more arrests in predicted areas → reinforces predictions

🏷️ Labeling Bias

Human annotators inject their biases into labels

Example: Different annotators may label "toxic" content inconsistently

🎯 Selection Bias

Non-random selection of training examples

Example: Medical AI trained only on patients who could afford treatment

🎯 COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm

Used in US courts to predict reoffending risk. ProPublica found it falsely labeled Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants.

📸 Facial Recognition Disparities

MIT study found commercial facial recognition had error rates of 0.8% for light-skinned men but 34.7% for dark-skinned women. Trained on non-diverse datasets.

💼 Amazon's Hiring Tool

Trained on 10 years of resumes (mostly male), it learned to penalize resumes containing words like "women's" and downgrade graduates of all-women's colleges. Amazon scrapped it.

🏥 Healthcare Algorithms

An algorithm used to allocate healthcare resources was found to systematically under-estimate the health needs of Black patients because it used healthcare costs (not actual health) as a proxy.

📊 Disaggregated Metrics

Don't just look at overall accuracy—break down performance by group

Check: Accuracy for each demographic group separately

📈 Data Audits

Examine your training data distribution

Check: Is each group well-represented?

⚖️ Fairness Metrics

Mathematical measures of fairness

Examples: Demographic parity, equalized odds, calibration

🧪 Counterfactual Testing

Change protected attributes, see if prediction changes

Test: Does changing gender in a resume change the score?

def check_fairness(y_true, y_pred, sensitive_attribute):
    """Check accuracy across different groups"""
    groups = set(sensitive_attribute)
    
    for group in groups:
        mask = sensitive_attribute == group
        group_accuracy = accuracy_score(y_true[mask], y_pred[mask])
        print(f"Group '{group}': {group_accuracy:.2%}")

📊 Pre-processing: Fix the Data

  • Collect diverse data: Ensure all groups are well-represented
  • Re-balance: Oversample underrepresented groups
  • Remove proxies: Exclude features that correlate with protected attributes

⚙️ In-processing: Fix the Model

  • Fairness constraints: Add fairness terms to the loss function
  • Adversarial debiasing: Train to be accurate but not predictive of protected attributes
  • Fair representation learning: Learn embeddings that don't encode bias

📤 Post-processing: Fix the Output

  • Threshold adjustment: Different decision thresholds per group
  • Calibration: Ensure predictions mean the same thing across groups
  • Reject option: Send uncertain cases to human review

👥 Process Improvements

  • Diverse teams: Different perspectives catch different biases
  • Community input: Involve affected communities in development
  • Regular audits: Continuously monitor for bias in production
  • Documentation: Model cards, datasheets, transparency

from fairlearn.metrics import MetricFrame
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_score

# Assume we have predictions and a sensitive feature (e.g., cat_food_type)
sensitive_feature = ["fancy", "regular", "fancy", "regular", ...]

# Create a MetricFrame to analyze metrics by group
metric_frame = MetricFrame(
    metrics={
        "accuracy": accuracy_score,
        "precision": precision_score
    },
    y_true=y_test,
    y_pred=y_pred,
    sensitive_features=sensitive_feature
)

# See metrics by group
print(metric_frame.by_group)
# Output:
#          accuracy  precision
# fancy       0.95       0.93
# regular     0.62       0.58  ← Big disparity!

# Overall metrics
print(f"Overall accuracy: {metric_frame.overall['accuracy']:.2%}")

# The difference shows our model is biased against regular brands! 🚨
